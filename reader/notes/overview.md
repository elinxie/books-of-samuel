# Overview: 1 & 2 Samuel

## Origin and title

In the Hebrew Bible, 1 and 2 Samuel form a single book, Samuel (שְׁמוּאֵל),
counted among the Neviʼim Rishonim ("Former Prophets") alongside Joshua,
Judges, and Kings. The division into two books originates with the Greek
Septuagint, which grouped Samuel and Kings into a single four-part history
it called *Basileiōn* ("Of Kingdoms" / "Of Reigns"), with Samuel forming
Books 1–2 and Kings forming Books 3–4. The Latin Vulgate followed this
division and this is the reason the King James Version carries the
alternate titles "The First/Second Book of the Kings" alongside "The
First/Second Book of Samuel." Hebrew manuscripts preserved the one-book
tradition considerably longer, and the two-book division was only carried
into printed Hebrew Bibles from the 16th century onward. The book is named
for Samuel, its opening figure, even though his death is recorded partway
through (1 Samuel 25) and he is not present for most of the narrative.

Jewish tradition (Babylonian Talmud, Bava Batra 14b–15a) attributes the
early chapters to Samuel himself, with the remainder completed by the
prophets Gad and Nathan — a division that lines up with the note at
1 Chronicles 29:29 that "the acts of David... are written in the
history/chronicles of Samuel the seer, Nathan the prophet, and Gad the
seer." Modern critical scholarship generally treats the book as a composite
work, woven together from older sources — often identified as including an
Ark narrative (1 Sam 4–6), a "History of David's Rise" (roughly 1 Sam
16 – 2 Sam 5), and a "Succession Narrative" or "Court History" (roughly
2 Sam 9–20, continuing into 1 Kings 1–2) — and shaped into its present form
by editors working within the wider Deuteronomistic history that runs from
Joshua through Kings.

## Narrative arc

The book traces Israel's transition from the loosely organized tribal
confederacy of the judges to a centralized monarchy, through three
overlapping lives: Samuel, the last of the judges and a prophet who anoints
Israel's first two kings; Saul, the first king, whose reign begins in
promise and ends in rejection and death; and David, the second king, whose
rise, reign, and family troubles occupy most of the remaining text. Read as
a whole, it moves from the barrenness and prayer of Hannah in its opening
chapter to the sins and sorrows of David's household in its closing
chapters, with the establishment of Jerusalem and the Davidic covenant as
its theological center of gravity.

## Outline

**1 Samuel**

- **1–7: Samuel.** Hannah's barrenness, vow, and prayer; the birth and
  dedication of Samuel; Eli's corrupt sons; Samuel's call as a boy in the
  tabernacle at Shiloh (ch. 3); the Philistines capture the Ark and Eli
  dies (ch. 4); the Ark among the Philistines and its return (ch. 5–6);
  Samuel's leadership and victory at Mizpah (ch. 7).
- **8–15: The rise and rejection of Saul.** Israel demands a king (ch. 8);
  Saul is anointed and confirmed (ch. 9–11); Samuel's farewell address
  (ch. 12); Saul's unlawful sacrifice (ch. 13); Jonathan's exploit at
  Michmash (ch. 14); Saul's incomplete destruction of the Amalekites and
  his rejection as king (ch. 15).
- **16–31: Saul and David.** David is anointed in secret (ch. 16); David
  and Goliath (ch. 17); David and Jonathan's covenant of friendship, and
  Saul's growing jealousy (ch. 18); Saul's repeated attempts on David's
  life and David's years as a fugitive (ch. 19–26); David among the
  Philistines (ch. 27, 29); Saul consults the medium at En-dor on the eve
  of battle (ch. 28); Saul and his sons die at the Battle of Gilboa
  (ch. 31).

**2 Samuel**

- **1–10: David's consolidation.** David's lament over Saul and Jonathan
  (ch. 1); David is made king, first over Judah and then over all Israel,
  after conflict with Saul's house (ch. 2–4); David captures Jerusalem and
  makes it his capital (ch. 5); the Ark is brought to Jerusalem (ch. 6);
  Nathan's oracle and the Davidic covenant (ch. 7); further military
  victories and administration (ch. 8–10).
- **11–20: Bathsheba and its consequences.** David's adultery with
  Bathsheba and the killing of Uriah (ch. 11); Nathan's rebuke and
  parable, and the death of the child (ch. 12); the rape of Tamar and its
  aftermath (ch. 13); Absalom's exile, return, and revolt (ch. 14–18),
  including David's flight from Jerusalem and Absalom's death; David's
  restoration and Sheba's revolt (ch. 19–20).
- **21–24: Epilogue.** A famine tied to Saul's treatment of the Gibeonites
  (ch. 21); David's song of deliverance, closely paralleling Psalm 18
  (ch. 22); David's "last words" and a roll of his mighty men (ch. 23);
  David's census, the resulting plague, and his purchase of Araunah's
  threshing floor — the future site of the Temple (ch. 24).

## Key figures

- **Samuel** — last of the judges, prophet, and kingmaker who anoints both Saul and David.
- **Eli** — priest and judge at Shiloh; his house is condemned for the sins of his sons.
- **Hannah** — Samuel's mother, whose prayer of thanksgiving (1 Sam 2) anticipates themes of reversal later echoed in the New Testament's Magnificat.
- **Saul** — Israel's first king, tall and initially humble, undone by impatience, disobedience, and jealousy.
- **Jonathan** — Saul's son, a skilled warrior whose loyalty to David crosses and ultimately costs him his claim to the throne.
- **David** — shepherd, musician, and warrior who becomes Israel's second king; the book's central figure from chapter 16 onward.
- **Michal, Abigail, Bathsheba** — David's wives, each central to a distinct episode in his rise and reign.
- **Nathan** — the court prophet who delivers both the dynastic covenant (2 Sam 7) and the rebuke over Bathsheba (2 Sam 12).
- **Joab** — David's ruthless military commander and nephew.
- **Absalom** — David's son whose revolt drives the central crisis of 2 Samuel's second half.
